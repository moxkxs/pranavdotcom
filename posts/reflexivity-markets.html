Reflexivity in Financial Markets

The concept of reflexivity suggests that participants' perceptions actively shape the reality they're trying to predict, creating feedback loops that traditional models fail to capture. George Soros first formalized this idea in his 1987 work The Alchemy of Finance, arguing that financial markets are inherently unstable because of the circular relationship between perception and reality.

The Feedback Loop

In classical economics, markets are assumed to tend toward equilibrium. Prices reflect fundamentals, and deviations are corrected by rational actors. Reflexivity challenges this directly: market participants don't just passively observe reality — they actively alter it through their beliefs and actions.

Consider a simple example. When investors believe a stock will rise, they buy it. The buying pressure pushes the price up, which confirms the original belief, which drives more buying. The "fundamental value" becomes entangled with the perception of value.

Financial markets, far from accurately reflecting all the available knowledge, always provide a distorted view of reality. The degree of distortion may vary from time to time. Sometimes it's negligible, at other times it's quite significant.

A Simple Model

We can express the reflexive relationship between price and fundamentals with a basic formulation. Let P(t) represent the market price at time t, and F(t) represent the underlying fundamental value:

P(t) = F(t) + β * ΔP(t-1) + ε(t)

where:
  β  = reflexivity coefficient (feedback strength)
  ΔP = price momentum (change from previous period)
  ε  = noise term

When β > 0, positive momentum feeds back into price, creating self-reinforcing trends. When β approaches 1, the system becomes unstable — small perturbations can cascade into bubbles or crashes.

Three Regimes of Market Behavior

Based on the strength of reflexive feedback, markets tend to operate in one of three regimes:

  1. Near-equilibrium — Reflexive effects are weak. Prices track fundamentals closely, and deviations are mean-reverting. Traditional models work reasonably well here.
  2. Far-from-equilibrium — Feedback loops dominate. Trend-following behavior overwhelms fundamental analysis, and prices can diverge significantly from intrinsic value.
  3. Regime transition — The critical phase where the market flips between states. These transitions are where the most violent price action occurs.

Implications for Systematic Trading

If reflexivity is real — and the evidence from every major bubble and crash suggests it is — then it has concrete implications for how we build trading systems:

  • Mean-reversion strategies should be conditional on regime. They work near equilibrium but blow up during reflexive episodes.
  • Momentum strategies are effectively betting on reflexivity. Their edge comes from the persistence of feedback loops.
  • Risk models that assume normal distributions will systematically underestimate tail risk during reflexive periods.
  • Position sizing matters more than signal quality during regime transitions.

An Empirical Look

Here's a rough Python sketch for detecting reflexive regimes using autocorrelation of returns:

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

def reflexivity_indicator(returns, window=60):
    """
    Measure reflexivity as the rolling autocorrelation
    of returns. High positive autocorrelation suggests
    self-reinforcing feedback loops.
    """
    rolling_autocorr = returns.rolling(window).apply(
        lambda x: x.autocorr(lag=1), raw=False
    )
    return rolling_autocorr

# Classify regimes
def classify_regime(autocorr, threshold=0.15):
    if abs(autocorr) < threshold:
        return "near_equilibrium"
    elif autocorr > threshold:
        return "reflexive_momentum"
    else:
        return "reflexive_reversal"

Closing Thoughts

The practical takeaway is that markets are not static systems with fixed parameters. They are adaptive, reflexive processes where the act of observation changes the thing being observed. Building systems that acknowledge this — that can detect when feedback loops are strengthening or weakening — is, I think, a genuine edge.
