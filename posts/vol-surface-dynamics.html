A deep dive into how implied volatility surfaces move and deform in response to market events. Examining the relationship between skew, term structure, and underlying price action.

What Is the Vol Surface?

The volatility surface is a three-dimensional representation of implied volatility as a function of two variables: strike price (or moneyness) and time to expiration. It's the market's way of telling you that Black-Scholes is wrong — or more precisely, that volatility is not constant across strikes and expirations.

The surface encodes an enormous amount of information about market expectations: tail risk pricing, supply-demand imbalances in the options market, and forward-looking views on realized volatility.

The Three Dimensions

Component What It Measures Key Driver
Level (ATM vol) Overall implied volatility Realized vol, VIX demand
Skew (slope) Asymmetry in strike-space Tail risk hedging demand
Term structure Vol across expirations Event calendars, mean reversion

Skew Dynamics

Equity skew exists because of structural demand: portfolio managers buy puts for protection, and market makers charge a premium for bearing that tail risk. But skew is not static. It steepens and flattens in predictable ways:

  • Spot down, skew up — As the market sells off, demand for downside protection spikes.
  • Spot up, skew flattens — In rallies, put demand eases and the surface compresses.
  • Vol-of-vol spikes — During dislocations, the entire surface becomes unstable.

Term Structure Regimes

Contango (normal) — longer-dated vol is higher than shorter-dated. This is the default state. Backwardation (inverted) — front-month vol exceeds back-month. This signals acute near-term stress.

def term_structure_slope(surface, front, back):
    """Positive = contango, Negative = backwardation"""
    front_atm = surface.get_atm_vol(front)
    back_atm = surface.get_atm_vol(back)
    return (back_atm - front_atm) / (back - front)
